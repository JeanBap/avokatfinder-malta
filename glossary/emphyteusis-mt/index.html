Emphyteusis

Emphyteusis is Malta's version of a long-term ground lease, where a landowner grants the right to use and improve land in exchange for a periodic ground rent (cens) for a fixed long term.

What Is Emphyteusis?

Emphyteusis — in Maltese, enfitewsi — is a real right under Maltese civil law that gives the holder (emphyteuta) the right to use and build on land owned by another person (the dominus) for an agreed long term, in exchange for a periodic payment called a cens (ground rent). It is not a lease in the ordinary sense — it is a real property right that can be mortgaged, inherited, and transferred.

Historical Prevalence in Malta

Emphyteusis was widespread in Malta's pre-independence era, particularly for agricultural land and Government-owned urban plots. Many old Maltese properties — especially in Valletta, Sliema, and the inner harbour areas — sit on land held under emphyteutical title rather than freehold. This means the building owner does not own the ground beneath it. Buyers must check the title carefully to understand whether they are buying freehold (propjeta' libera) or emphyteutical rights.

Types: Temporary and Perpetual

Maltese law recognises both temporary emphyteusis (for a fixed term after which the land and all improvements revert to the dominus) and perpetual emphyteusis (continuing indefinitely, resembling freehold in practice). Temporary emphyteusis with a remaining term of fewer than 20–30 years raises significant valuation and mortgageability challenges.

Ground Rent (Cens) and Redemption

The periodic cens payable under emphyteusis may be nominal (a historical artefact of a low figure set decades ago) or substantial. Maltese law has allowed emphyteutas to redeem their title by capitalising the cens — paying a lump sum to convert emphyteutical title to freehold. The redemption price is typically calculated by the government. If an emphyteutical property is being sold, both parties should confirm whether redemption has occurred or the cens is current.

Mortgage Implications

Maltese banks can lend against emphyteutical properties but assess the remaining term carefully. A bank will typically not mortgage an emphyteutical property with fewer than 20–25 years remaining beyond the mortgage term. Buyers relying on a mortgage to purchase an emphyteutical property should confirm the bank's policy before exchanging the promise of sale.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Buying an old Maltese town-house assuming it is freehold without checking the title. Many inner-harbour properties are emphyteutical — check the title with your notary before signing anything.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: If you are buying an emphyteutical property, ask the notary to calculate the redemption price before signing the promise of sale — converting to freehold at or shortly after purchase often adds significant value.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is emphyteutical property cheaper than freehold?

Yes, typically — the discount reflects the title complexity and potential reversion risk.

Can an emphyteutical property be inherited?

Yes — emphyteusis is a heritable real right.

What happens at the end of a temporary emphyteusis?

The land and all improvements on it revert to the dominus — this is a major risk for short-term emphyteusis.
