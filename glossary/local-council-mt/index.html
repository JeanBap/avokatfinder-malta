Local Council Malta

Malta's 68 local councils manage community services, collect local rates, and issue permits for minor works — buyers should check for unpaid rates and local notices before completing a purchase.

Role of Local Councils

Malta is divided into 68 local councils (kunsilli lokali), each responsible for basic municipal services in its town or village: street maintenance, minor landscaping, refuse collection, and community facilities. Local councils also collect local kunsilli rates — a small annual charge per residential unit. While the rates are modest, unpaid amounts can accumulate and constitute a charge on the property that the buyer inherits.

Local Rate Clearance

Before signing the deed of sale, the notary should obtain or request confirmation from the local council that all rates are paid up to date. In practice, this is sometimes overlooked for small transactions, but it is best practice. A seller who owes local council rates must settle them from the sale proceeds — the notary can retain an amount equivalent to the outstanding debt if the seller does not pay before the deed.

Minor Development Notifications

Some minor works that do not require a full Planning Authority permit may still require notification to the local council (for example, works affecting pavements or street-facing elements). The local council has limited powers to object but can flag issues to the PA. Understanding the local council's position on neighbourhood development is part of the context-setting due diligence for any development project.

Heritage and Urban Conservation Areas

Several Maltese local councils administer their own heritage lists of protected facades and streetscapes in addition to the PA's national-level protections. In historic villages and Valletta, local council input is sought for planning applications affecting traditional buildings. Buyers planning changes to facades or apertures should consult both the PA and the local council early in the design process.

Community Character and Amenities

Beyond formal obligations, the local council is a good informal source of information about planned infrastructure (road works, new schools, transport hubs), neighbourhood plans, and community character. Attending a local council meeting before committing to a property in an unfamiliar village can give insight into local priorities and any contentious development proposals nearby.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Ignoring unpaid local council rates. They are small but they are a charge on the property — a buyer who completes without checking may inherit arrears with interest.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Call the local council directly (most now have websites and email) to ask whether there are any outstanding rates or notices on the property — it takes five minutes and is not routinely done by all notaries.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are local council rates included in utility bills?

No — they are a separate annual notice, typically sent to the registered property address.

Can a local council block my PA permit?

Not directly — but they can submit an objection during consultation, which the PA must consider.

How much are typical local council rates?

Usually €100–€300 per year for a residential unit — amounts vary by council.
