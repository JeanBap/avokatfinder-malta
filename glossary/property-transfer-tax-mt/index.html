Property Transfer Tax

Property transfer tax (PTT) in Malta is an 8% final withholding tax levied on the seller based on the sale price, replacing capital gains tax on immovable property disposals.

How PTT Works

Malta replaced capital gains tax on immovable property with a property transfer tax (PTT), a flat-rate final withholding tax deducted by the notary from the seller's proceeds and remitted directly to the Commissioner for Revenue. The standard rate is 8% of the sale price. Because PTT is withheld at source, the seller never sees that portion of the proceeds — it is an automatic deduction.

Reduced Rates and Exemptions

Several categories attract reduced PTT rates: transfers of property acquired before 1 January 2004 (where the gain is computed under an old regime at 5%); principal residences sold by owners who have lived there for at least 3 consecutive years and the sale is within 1 year of ceasing to use it as primary residence (12% on the gain rather than 8% on the price — sometimes more favourable); and transfers by gift in direct line (reduced or nil rate). Always compute the effective tax under each regime to choose the most favourable.

Comparison with Capital Gains Option

A seller who believes their actual gain is small relative to the sale price may apply to be taxed on the actual capital gain at 15% rather than 8% of the gross sale price. This gain-based option requires filing documentation proving the acquisition cost and improvements. It is worth computing for sellers who bought at a price close to the current sale price, as 15% of a small gain may be less than 8% of the full price.

Notary's Withholding Role

The notary collects PTT from the seller's proceeds at the deed stage and is legally responsible for remitting it to the Commissioner within prescribed timeframes. Sellers should confirm with their notary well in advance what amount will be withheld so they can plan the net proceeds available for purchasing their next property or settling debts.

Non-Resident Sellers

Non-resident sellers are subject to the same PTT framework, but must also consider whether their home country taxes the gain under their domestic rules (with credit for Maltese tax under a double tax treaty, where applicable). Malta has a broad treaty network — non-resident sellers should take bilateral tax advice before agreeing the sale price and timeline.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Selling without modelling both the 8% price-based PTT and the 15% gain-based option. For a property bought at near-current market value, the gain-based option saves significant tax.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Gather all original purchase documentation, notary receipts, and improvement invoices years before you plan to sell — you need them to support a gain-based PTT election.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is PTT in addition to stamp duty?

PTT is the seller's tax; stamp duty is the buyer's tax — they are separate and both apply.

What if I inherit a property and then sell it?

Inherited property uses the probate value as the acquisition cost — seek advice on whether the price-based or gain-based PTT rate is lower.

Can PTT be refunded if I overpay?

A refund can be claimed from the Commissioner if the applicable rate was incorrectly calculated.
