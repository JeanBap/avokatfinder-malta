Affiliation Order

A court order establishing the paternity of a child and determining parental rights and child support obligations.

Definition

An affiliation order is a judicial determination that establishes the paternity (or legal fatherhood) of a child and sets out the rights and obligations of the alleged father toward the child. In Malta, affiliation orders are governed by the Family Law Act and are an important tool for establishing legal paternity, determining custody and access rights, and ensuring that children receive financial support from both parents. Affiliation orders apply regardless of whether the parents were married, and they provide the legal foundation for child support obligations and inheritance rights.

The process of obtaining an affiliation order in Malta typically begins with the mother filing a petition in the Family Court, alleging that a specific man is the father of the child. The alleged father has the right to contest the claim, and the matter proceeds to a hearing where evidence is presented. Modern affiliation orders often rely on DNA testing to establish paternity with a high degree of certainty. If DNA evidence conclusively establishes paternity, courts typically grant the affiliation order and proceed to determine matters of custody, access, and financial support.

Once an affiliation order is granted, the father is legally recognized as the child's parent and assumes parental rights and responsibilities. These include the obligation to provide financial support for the child, typically through monthly child support payments determined by the court. The amount of child support is calculated based on the father's income, the child's needs, and other relevant factors. The father may also acquire custody or access rights, depending on the circumstances and the best interests of the child.

An affiliation order can be challenged or appealed if there is evidence of fraud, false testimony, or if new evidence becomes available that contradicts the previous finding. For example, if DNA evidence that was not available at the time of the original order later reveals that the man is not the biological father, the order can be appealed. However, once an affiliation order becomes final and a reasonable period has passed, courts are generally reluctant to overturn it due to the importance of legal certainty in family relationships.

Affiliation orders serve as an important protection for children, ensuring they have access to support from both parents and establishing their legal status and inheritance rights. They also protect society by ensuring that individuals who father children take financial responsibility for their upbringing. For unmarried parents and children born outside of wedlock, affiliation orders are a critical tool for establishing legal parentage and securing the rights and protections that formal family relationships provide.

Key Facts

  • An affiliation order establishes paternity and parental rights and obligations.
  • The process typically involves filing a petition and presenting evidence, often including DNA testing.
  • Once granted, the father has obligations to provide financial support through child support payments.
  • The father may acquire custody or access rights based on the best interests of the child.
  • Affiliation orders can be challenged with new evidence, but are generally protected from reversal after final determination.

Common Mistake

Unmarried parents delay establishing an affiliation order, leaving the child without clear legal paternity and inheritance rights.

Expert Tip

Seek an affiliation order early and obtain DNA testing to establish paternity conclusively and resolve potential disputes.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is paternity established in an affiliation order?

Paternity is established through evidence presented at court, typically including DNA testing, which provides a high degree of certainty.

What are the financial obligations following an affiliation order?

The father is obligated to provide monthly child support determined by the court based on his income and the child's needs.

Can an affiliation order be challenged after it is granted?

Yes, an affiliation order can be challenged with new evidence, such as DNA evidence that contradicts the original finding, though courts are reluctant to overturn final orders.
