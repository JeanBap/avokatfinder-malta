Deed of Sale

The deed of sale (att ta' bejgh) is Malta's final notarial instrument that legally transfers ownership of immovable property from seller to buyer.

What Is a Deed of Sale?

The deed of sale (Maltese: att ta' bejgh) is the formal notarial document that completes a property transaction in Malta. Executed before a civil law notary, it transfers legal title from seller to buyer. The notary reads the deed aloud to both parties, confirms their understanding and consent, and then both parties sign. The notary certifies the deed and it is then registered with the Public Registry.

What the Notary Verifies

Before drafting the deed, the notary carries out a title search at the Public Registry to confirm the seller's ownership and check for any encumbrances, hypothecs (mortgages), or judicial annotations. The notary also verifies that all taxes and dues on the property are paid, that there are no pending enforcement notices from the Planning Authority, and that the parties have legal capacity to contract.

Stamp Duty at Completion

Stamp duty of 5% of the purchase price is collected by the notary at the deed stage and remitted to the Commissioner for Revenue. The 1% paid at the promise of sale stage is deducted, leaving a net 4% to pay at deed. First-time buyers who qualify for relief may pay a reduced rate on the first €200,000 of value.

Property Transfer Tax

The seller pays a final withholding tax on the transfer: generally 8% of the sale price (5% for certain transfers of property held for 5+ years prior to 2004 or under other relief provisions). This is the seller's capital gains equivalent and is withheld by the notary from the sale proceeds. Sellers should model this cost before agreeing the net price.

Registration and Effect

Once signed, the notary registers the deed at the Public Registry and the Land Registry (for registered title). The buyer is the legal owner from the moment of signing, but the registration confirms priority against subsequent claims. Registration typically takes days to weeks depending on workload at the Registry.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Assuming the notary acts exclusively in your interests. In Malta, one notary often represents both parties — consider appointing your own notary or lawyer to review the deed.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Budget for stamp duty and legal fees well before the deed date — the 4% net stamp duty on a €350,000 property is €14,000 due on the day of signing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who pays stamp duty — buyer or seller?

Stamp duty is the buyer's obligation; property transfer tax is the seller's obligation.

How long after promise of sale is the deed signed?

Typically 3–6 months, as agreed in the konvenju.

Can I sign the deed by power of attorney?

Yes — a notarially authenticated power of attorney allows a representative to sign on your behalf.
