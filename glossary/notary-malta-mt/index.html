Notary Malta

A Maltese notary (nutar) is a civil law professional who authenticates property deeds, conducts title searches, and acts as a neutral intermediary for both buyer and seller in real estate transactions.

The Notary's Role in Malta

In Malta's civil law system, a notary (nutar in Maltese) is a legally qualified professional with a public function: to authenticate documents and give them full evidentiary weight. In property transactions, the notary drafts and authenticates both the promise of sale (konvenju) and the final deed of sale (att ta' bejgh), conducts the title search, calculates duties, and registers the deed at the Public Registry.

Notary as Neutral Party

Unlike a lawyer who acts exclusively for one client, a notary has a duty to both buyer and seller. The notary must ensure the transaction is legal and that neither party is disadvantaged by the deed's terms. However, this duty does not extend to giving commercial negotiation advice — the notary protects legal validity, not commercial interests. If you have specific concerns or complex circumstances, engaging a separate property lawyer in addition to the notary is advisable.

Choosing a Notary in Malta

Any Maltese notary can act in any property transaction — there is no geographic restriction. The choice often comes down to language (Maltese, English, Italian), specialisation (some notaries focus on commercial transactions, others on residential), and personal recommendation. The Chamber of Advocates and Notaries maintains a register. Referrals from your bank or a trusted estate agent are common starting points.

Fees

Notary fees in Malta are not regulated by a fixed tariff — they are negotiated between notary and client. Typical fees for a standard residential transaction range from 1–2% of the property price for the full service (promise of sale, title search, deed of sale, registration). Disbursements (stamp duty, registry fees) are collected additionally. Always agree the fee schedule in writing before the notary begins work.

Title Search and Liability

The notary is liable for a negligent title search — if a hypothec or defect in title is missed due to the notary's negligence and the buyer suffers loss, the notary's professional indemnity insurance covers the claim. This makes the notary's role critically important: they are the primary safeguard against title defects in a Maltese transaction.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Using the seller's notary without any independent legal advice. While the notary is technically neutral, their pre-existing relationship with the seller can create practical bias. Consider engaging your own lawyer for complex deals.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Agree the notary's total fee scope (including title search depth, number of titles, and registry registrations) in writing at the outset — oral agreements are hard to enforce when disputed later.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use a foreign notary for a Malta property purchase?

No — Maltese notarial deeds must be executed by a Maltese notary.

Does the notary give tax advice?

No — tax planning is separate. Consult a Malta tax adviser alongside the notary.

What if the notary makes a mistake in the deed?

Corrections to registered deeds require a rectification deed — also notarised and re-registered. Errors caused by negligence may give rise to a claim against the notary.
