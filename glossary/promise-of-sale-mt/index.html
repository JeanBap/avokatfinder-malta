Promise of Sale

The promise of sale (known as a 'konvenju' in Maltese) is the preliminary agreement between buyer and seller in Malta, locking in the price and terms before the final deed of sale.

What Is a Promise of Sale?

In Malta, a promise of sale — locally called a konvenju — is the bilateral agreement signed between buyer and seller that commits both parties to complete the transaction at an agreed price and on agreed terms. It is signed before a Maltese notary and is legally binding: a party who withdraws without cause may face a claim for damages or specific performance.

Standard Content

A typical promise of sale in Malta records: the full description of the property (including its mapping reference and Local Plan classification); the agreed purchase price; the deposit amount and the conditions under which it is forfeitable; suspensive conditions (typically mortgage approval and architect's due diligence); and the deadline for the final deed of sale — usually 3 months, though up to 6 is common for complex transactions or off-plan purchases.

The Deposit

A deposit of 10% of the purchase price is customary at the promise of sale stage. This is usually held by the seller's notary in a client account. If the buyer withdraws without a valid contractual reason, the seller typically retains the deposit. If the seller withdraws, the buyer is entitled to receive double the deposit back — a strong deterrent against seller cold feet in a rising market.

Suspensive Conditions

Most promises of sale include at least one suspensive condition: typically mortgage approval from a Maltese bank within a defined period. If the condition is not satisfied within the deadline, the contract lapses and the deposit is returned. Buyers should ensure that any known planning issues or title defects are also included as conditions to be cleared before the deed date.

Stamp Duty Implications

In Malta, stamp duty on property transfers is primarily collected at the final deed stage. However, the promise of sale itself triggers a stamp duty obligation of 1% of the purchase price (treated as a payment on account of the full 5% stamp duty due at completion). This must be paid within 21 days of the promise of sale being signed.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Agreeing to a promise of sale without a suspensive condition for mortgage approval. If the bank declines, you may lose your deposit unless the contract specifically protects you.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Always check that the property's planning status (any outstanding enforcement orders or development permits) is covered by a suspensive condition in the promise of sale before signing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a promise of sale mandatory in Malta?

It is not legally mandatory but is universal practice — the final deed almost always follows a konvenju.

Can I withdraw if I change my mind?

Yes, but you forfeit your deposit unless a valid suspensive condition applies.

Is the 1% stamp duty at promise stage refunded?

It is credited against the 5% total stamp duty due at the final deed stage — it is not an additional cost.
