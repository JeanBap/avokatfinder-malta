Property Search Malta

A property search in Malta is the formal due-diligence process conducted by the notary at the Public Registry and Planning Authority to verify title, uncover encumbrances, and confirm planning status before a transaction completes.

What a Property Search Covers

A property search in Malta is not a single query but a bundle of concurrent checks carried out (or commissioned) by the notary prior to the deed of sale. The searches typically include: a title search at the Public Registry (tracing the chain of ownership and checking for hypothecs); a planning search at the Malta Planning Authority (checking permits, conditions, and enforcement notices); a local council check (for unpaid local rates or objections); and a court register search (checking for judicial annotations or pending proceedings affecting the property).

Public Registry Title Search

The core of any Maltese property search is the Public Registry title search, which traces ownership back at least 30 years and identifies all registered encumbrances. For complex properties (subdivisions, inherited titles, emphyteutical rights), the search may need to go back much further. The notary may engage a search agent to review the physical archive for very old or complex titles.

Planning Authority Search

The PA search (via the online Planning Authority portal) reveals: all permit applications (approved, pending, refused); any conditions attached to approved permits; and any enforcement notices. This search is critical for any property that has been extended or modified — confirming that all physical works correspond to approved permits prevents the buyer from inheriting enforcement obligations.

Timing of Searches

Searches are typically carried out between the promise of sale and the deed of sale, giving time for issues to be resolved. However, a preliminary PA search can and should be done before the promise of sale is signed — if a significant enforcement notice exists, the buyer should know before committing. Searches ordered only just before the deed date leave no time to address problems.

Results and Warranties

The notary shares the search results with both parties. If encumbrances are found, the seller is typically required to discharge them before or at the deed stage. The promise of sale should contain a warranty that title will be free of undisclosed encumbrances at the deed date, giving the buyer recourse if a defect only emerges between the two signing dates.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Leaving the PA search until the last week before the deed. An enforcement notice discovered at that stage derails the transaction — early searching is essential.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Commission a preliminary PA printout (it is free and available online) the day you identify a property you like. Thirty seconds of searching can save months of wasted due diligence.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I do a property search myself?

A PA search is publicly available online. The Public Registry title search requires notarial access but the notary does this as part of their standard service.

How much does a property search cost?

Registry fees are modest; the notary's time for the search is usually included in their overall fee.

What if the search reveals an unknown mortgage?

The seller must discharge it before the deed. If they cannot, the buyer has grounds to withdraw and recover the deposit.
