AIP Permit

An AIP (Acquisition of Immovable Property) permit is required by non-EU nationals — and in some cases EU citizens — before they can purchase property in Malta.

What Is an AIP Permit?

Malta restricts property purchases by certain foreign nationals under the Acquisition of Immovable Property (AIP) Act. Non-EU/EEA nationals purchasing property in Malta for personal use (not investment or business) must obtain an AIP permit from the Ministry for Finance before they can legally own the property. The permit confirms the government's consent to the acquisition.

Who Needs an AIP Permit?

Non-EU nationals always require an AIP permit for any property purchase in Malta. EU citizens who have not been resident in Malta for at least 5 continuous years also require an AIP permit if they are buying a secondary property — their first property as a primary residence is exempt. EU nationals with 5+ years of continuous Maltese residency are treated as Maltese nationals and need no permit.

Special Designated Areas

Properties in Malta's Special Designated Areas (SDAs) — including Portomaso, Tigne Point, Pender Place, Cottonera, Tas-Sellum, SmartCity Malta, and Fort Cambridge — are entirely exempt from AIP requirements. Any foreign national (EU or non-EU) can buy freely in SDAs, making them popular with international investors. However, SDA properties also command a price premium precisely because of this unrestricted access.

Application Process

The AIP application is submitted to the Residency Malta Agency or the Ministry for Finance, with supporting documents: identity papers, proof of funds, a description of the intended property, and payment of the application fee. Processing typically takes 4–8 weeks. The promise of sale is usually signed before the permit is obtained, with the AIP approval as a suspensive condition.

Conditions and Limitations

An AIP permit typically restricts the buyer to using the property as a personal residence — it cannot be rented out or used commercially without further authorisation. The permit is property-specific and does not transfer if the buyer later purchases a different property. Each new acquisition requires a fresh permit.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Signing a promise of sale for a non-SDA property without including AIP approval as a suspensive condition. If the permit is refused, you lose your deposit unless the contract protects you.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: If you want maximum flexibility as a non-EU buyer, target SDA properties — no AIP needed, no usage restrictions, and strong resale liquidity among international buyers.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can an AIP property be rented out?

Generally no — the permit authorises personal use. Check with the notary before renting.

Does the AIP restrict which property I can buy?

Yes — it is specific to the property described in the application. Each new purchase needs a new permit.

How much does an AIP permit cost?

The application fee is modest (under €500) but check the current schedule with the relevant authority.
