Condominium Malta

A condominium in Malta is a building with multiple separately owned units and shared common areas, governed by the Condominium Act and administered by a collectively elected administrator.

Legal Framework

Malta's Condominium Act (Cap. 398) governs the ownership and management of multi-unit buildings where separate floors or apartments are individually owned and common areas (roof, stairwells, lifts, external walls, foundations) are held collectively. Every owner in the building is automatically a member of the condominium and has rights and obligations in proportion to their unit's share of the total building.

The Condominium Administrator

The Act requires each condominium to elect an administrator (in larger buildings, a professional is typically appointed) who manages the common areas, arranges maintenance, holds the common fund, and convenes the annual general meeting. The administrator is the legal representative of the condominium and can enter contracts for repairs, insurance, and services on behalf of all owners.

Service Charges and Common Fund

Each owner contributes to a common fund proportional to their unit share. This fund covers maintenance, insurance of the building, shared utility costs, lift maintenance, and the administrator's fee. Before buying, request at least 2 years' meeting minutes, the current budget, and the common fund balance — an underfunded common fund or large planned expenditure not yet levied is a hidden liability for the incoming buyer.

Majority Decisions

Most day-to-day decisions (routine maintenance, appointing contractors) require a simple majority. Major works or significant expenditure above a threshold typically require a two-thirds majority. Unanimous consent is required for works that alter the structure or affect private rights. A single blocking owner can stall urgent repairs — understand the decision-making dynamics of the building before buying.

Short-Term Rentals

The condominium rules may restrict or ban Airbnb-style short-term rentals. While Malta permits short-term holiday lettings under a separate regulatory framework (Malta Tourism Authority licence), a condominium's internal rules can prohibit them. Check the deed of condominium before purchasing for investment purposes.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Buying an apartment without checking the common fund balance. A building with zero reserves and a leaking roof means a large special levy for the new owner shortly after completion.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Attend the building's annual general meeting as a prospective buyer (ask the seller or agent for permission) — the discussions reveal real issues that formal documents may downplay.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I be forced to pay for repairs I disagree with?

Yes — a duly passed majority resolution binds all owners, including those who voted against.

Is the administrator liable for mismanagement?

Yes — the administrator has a fiduciary duty and can be sued for negligence or breach of that duty.

What if the condominium has no administrator?

Any owner can apply to a court to appoint one — an unmanaged building is a legal risk and a practical headache.
